Where Does Our Data Come From?

Capitol League pulls from nonpartisan, public data with a simple rule: .gov > .edu > .org > everything else. Here’s the short list of sources we rely on.

Primary · .gov Secondary · .edu Context · .org / other
We always prefer official U.S. government publications. Other sources are used for context and cross-checks, not to override official records.
.gov — Official U.S. Government Data

Congress.gov (Library of Congress)

Primary source for bills, summaries, status, and roll-call links.

Used for: bill titles, short descriptions, sponsors/co-sponsors, and the official lifecycle of legislation in Capitol League.

Visit congress.gov

Clerk of the U.S. House — Roll Call Votes

Official House vote tallies by member and party.

Used for: detailed House vote breakdowns that feed our scoreboards and voting stats for individual members.

Visit clerk.house.gov/Votes

U.S. Senate Vote Records

Official Senate roll-call votes and legislative actions.

Used for: Senate vote results and member-level stats, generally accessed via Senate.gov and linked pages on Congress.gov.

Visit senate.gov vote records

GovInfo (Government Publishing Office)

Official publications from all three branches of government.

Used for: deeper reference documents like the Congressional Record, public laws, Federal Register entries, and budget documents that support rules text and special event cards.

Visit govinfo.gov

U.S. Census · My Congressional District & APIs

Demographic and economic data at the district level.

Used for: district “flavor” stats (population, income, urban/rural mix) that appear on district-focused cards and scenarios.

Visit census.gov/mycd

Data.gov

Catalog of federal datasets across agencies.

Used for: additional contextual datasets (e.g., spending, programs, and indicators) that support long-term or special scenario cards.

Visit data.gov
.edu — Educational & Research Guides

University Government Info Guides

Nonpartisan explainers from university libraries and civics programs.

Used for: background explanations of how Congress works (e.g., committees, cloture, reconciliation). We treat these as teaching aids and cross-check them against official .gov sources.

View example .edu guides
.org & Other — Context & Visualization

EveryCRSReport.com

Public mirror of Congressional Research Service reports.

Used for: locating CRS reports that help us “translate” complex policy into plain-language summaries for players. Official versions are always verified against Congress.gov when available.

Visit everycrsreport.com

C-SPAN & related archives

Video coverage of floor debates, hearings, and events.

Used for: real-world examples and quotes that inspire event cards and narrative moments. Official records still come from .gov sources.

Visit c-span.org

OpenSecrets & similar transparency projects

Campaign finance, lobbying, and influence data.

Used for: optional context on money in politics, sometimes reflected in special card mechanics. Data is cross-checked and not treated as a replacement for official filings.

Visit opensecrets.org

Ballotpedia & other civic encyclopedias

Neutral-tone summaries of offices, elections, and procedures.

Used for: quick checks on terminology and structure. Final rules and mechanics are always aligned to primary .gov sources.

Visit ballotpedia.org
Accuracy note: Capitol League is a game, not a legal resource. When in doubt, official U.S. government publications control, and we will adjust our data and rules to match them.
